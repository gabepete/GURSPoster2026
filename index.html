Citation Networks of Suspected Predatory Publishers in Nursing Literature

J. Walker (1), G Peterson (1), E. Wheeles (2), M. Quazi (3), H Carter-Templeton (3)

(1) NCCU, (2) University of Missouri, Saint Louis, (3) West Virginia University School of Nursing

Abstract

  • Predatory publishing poses a growing threat to scientific integrity.
  • This study investigates structural differences in citation networks of nursing literature.
  • Legitimate journals are embedded in larger, more diverse ecosystems.

Research Question

Do citation networks of suspected predatory publishers exhibit structurally different patterns from those of legitimate publishers?

Hypothesis: There is a quantifiable difference in clustering and connectivity.

Methods

  • 60 articles per group (Predatory vs. Legitimate)
  • Sources: Cabell’s, Oermann (2016), and INANE
  • Data: OpenAlex citation records
  • Analysis: 1–3 hop network expansion

    • Using the OpenAlex database, a Python script computed clustering coefficients and constructed up to 3rd-degree (hop) ego citation maps to characterize each group's citation ecosystem.

Results

Citations / article20 vs 6
2-hop network size47k vs 12k
Communities76 vs 55

Legitimate articles averaged ~20 references vs. ~6 for predatory articles. At 2 hops, the legitimate network was roughly four times larger.

Conclusion

Citation network structure meaningfully distinguishes articles from legitimate publishers versus suspected predatory ones. Bibliometric analysis could serve as an automated tool for flagging potentially predatory publications.

Discussion

Analyzing 2nd and 3rd-hop citation networks reveals the broader scholarly community. Legitimate articles sit within a large, well-connected research community, whereas predatory articles engage with a smaller, more insular slice of literature. This points to qualitative differences in how articles are integrated into scientific discourse.
